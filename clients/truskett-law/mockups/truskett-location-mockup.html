Tulsa Has Its Own Set of Hazards

Tulsa is a large, spread-out city, and that geography creates real injury risk every day.

The Gathering Place on Riverside Drive draws thousands of visitors each week. The park’s access points along Riverside are some of the busiest pedestrian zones in the city. Drivers moving quickly on that corridor and pedestrians crossing between parking areas have created a pattern of conflicts. If you or a family member was hit by a vehicle near the riverfront, that context matters when building your case.

Tulsa also has an aging housing stock. Roughly 45 percent of homes in the city were built before 1970. Older properties are more likely to have deteriorating walkways, worn staircases, and outdated structural conditions. Slip and fall injuries on poorly maintained rental properties and commercial spaces are common claims our team handles.

The Historic Greenwood District and other dense parts of northeast Tulsa see heavy commercial vehicle traffic. Semi-trucks and delivery vehicles navigate streets that were not always designed for their size. Collisions involving large trucks cause some of the most serious injuries we see.

What a Tulsa Personal Injury Claim Looks Like

Personal injury law covers situations where one party’s negligence causes harm to another. In Oklahoma, injured people generally have two years from the date of injury to file a lawsuit. Talking to an Insurance Company Before You Talk to a Lawyer

Insurance adjusters work for the insurance company, not for you. Their job is to close your claim for as little as possible. Recorded statements made early can be used to limit what you recover later.

Before you give a statement or sign anything, it is worth a free conversation with a personal injury attorney who can tell you what your case may actually be worth.
